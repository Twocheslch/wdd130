Client Report:

  • I decided on the images and content to use by creating a theme for my website and ensuring that everything in it served to propagate that theme. In this case, it was a Chinese restaurant menu. So, since it was a menu, I would need pictures of food. Since it was Chinese, it would have to be Chinese food. As for finding/creating the images, I asked ChatGPT to create them so that my lead developer would only have to fit in one reference for one source. As for the written content, I looked up a bunch of images of real menus, took inspiration from their descriptions of food, and decided to do something similar for myself, while also incorporating some allergy warnings to really make it feel authentic.
  • I communicated with my developer over text message. Given that, in an ideal world, the junior devs would contact the lead dev if they had any questions, then the lead dev would only contact me, a one-on-one communication method seemed fitting. And it did fit well. There were zero communication issues, and we talked freely throughout the whole creation process.

Junior Developer Report:

  • I communicated with both of my team leads through Microsoft Teams. Luckily, there were little to no issues here.
  • During the creation of my pages, I always felt like I had a good sense of what was expected of me, and had good guidance on where and how to build my pages.
  • Thanks to these clear directives, I really felt like I was able to make large, meaningful contributions to the website as a whole, and to the webpages I was making myself.
  • I did run into one issue with GitHub while creating my subpage for Lettie Waite. The other junior developer had capitalized the first letter of the name of the images folder, as well as all the names of the images within. This created an issue where, if I hosted the website on my computer (which is running Windows), the images would load fine, but wouldn’t when looking at the page on GitHub. Having realized my mistake, I quickly renamed the folder and images. But it still didn’t work! It turns out that I can’t make a commit just to change the capitalization of a file. So, instead, I entirely renamed everything, with the correct capitalization (none) in use. It’s also important to note that, since the lead developer for this site wasn’t responding, there was no index page that I could mess up inadvertently by renaming everything.

General Experience and Reflections

  • The most challenging part of this project was just starting it. At a glance, this seemed like a huge project, and it was quite daunting. But once I started, I realized that I could get all of my projects done quickly and professionally. The second greates challenge I faced was trying to communicate with people who would not respond. Thankfully, Brother Philips did a great job informing us that we were (mostly) only responsible for our own pages, and as long as we tried our best, we would not be held accountable for someone else not doing their work, so I was never worried.
  • What I enjoyed most was either getting to see my page completed (it looks beautiful) or getting to work on other projects that made me think outside of the box. Since you usually have an idea of how you’re going to make a website when you’re making the plan for it, you can make plans that don’t challenge you, and only play to your strengths. Getting to receive plans from someone else really made me think in new ways, and I enjoyed that.
  • If I got to do this project again, I would try to just start earlier. Even if I were only working on it for an hour a day, I would have gotten it done without having to resort to crunch time.
  • After this project, I feel much more confident to work on real-world projects. I feel that, since I was able to act in every role, I got to experience everything. In this way, I was able to conquer the fear of the unknown, the only fear to exist in every human on the planet.
